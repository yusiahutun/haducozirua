Behind the gates of the Tunisia hotel where an ISIS gunman murdered 39 holidaymakers

 Posted on March 30, 2024  |  12 minutes  |  2501 words  |  Larita Shotwell

The hotel is silent: the reception desk empty, the perfectly-placed chairs covered, the beach empty, the sun loungers which used to line it nowhere to be seen.

A few inches of stagnant, green water sits in the bottom of the abandoned swimming pool. The gym equipment sits unused, gathering dust. The doors to its luxurious suites, closed.

This is what remains of the Riu Imperial Marhaba, once a much-loved resort, now tarnished by terror.

For it was here that the sounds of shooting echoed through the pool area, that the screams of the dying rose from the beach and explosions ricocheted along the corridor as the Tunisian gunman carried out his murderous rampage.

But the closed doors and empty rooms are not just the fate of the Riu Imperial Marhaba. All along this stretch of beach, and across Tunisia, hotels are shutting their doors, the tourists they relied on too fearful to make the flight across the Mediterranean.

Last year, just 5.5million people visited the country - down 25 per cent from the year before. At the start of 2015, it looked like it was going to be the country's strongest year for tourism yet.

And then on June 26, 2015 - a year ago on Sunday - ISIS killer Seifeddine Rezgui Yacoubi murdered 38 tourists, including 30 British citizens, as they relaxed on holiday.

Rezgui's half hour of terror, starting shortly before midday local time, when he stepped foot on the beach, in the popular resort of Port El Kantaoui, with his AK-47 assault rifle concealed in a parasol.

First, he picked off the people sunbathing on the loungers, laid out along the sand, each with their own yellow towel. Many were pensioners, unable to flee as the bullets began to fly and the screams rang out.

Afterwards, he headed towards the Imperial, sending people scrabbling out of the pool as he continued to pick them off.

He made it all the way into the hotel reception area, where he let off grenades, killing one of his youngest victims, Carly Lovett, who was just 24. 

Survivors said Rezgui, who was high on cocaine at the time, laughed and smiled as he massacred dozens of victims during the 25-minute rampage.

Paul Short, from Northumberland, saved fellow holiday-makers after making sure his wife, Sarah, was safe. He had assumed the sound of gunfire was fireworks but soon realised his and Sarah's lives were in mortal danger.

He gave aid to one man who was bleeding heavily after he was shot. He told ITV's Good Morning Britain: 'I shoved the towel into his leg and put a tourniquet on him - he was more concerned about his wife, but his wife was lying above him and she was already dead and I didn't want to tell him.'

The former Territorial soldier was forced to tell the man that his wife was dead. He moved her body with another hotel guest as the gunman took pictures of the scenes of terror. 

Mr Short added that a deaf man started walking to Rezgui calling for his wife. 

'The gunman turned around and started firing at him, I ran up and took him behind the pillar while the gunman shot at him, taking out the coffee table ... he started running up to the two of us,' Mr Short said. 

Mr Short rescued the deaf man and managed to take cover under a settee before the gunman threw a grenade.

Witnesses described hearing bullets 'whizzing past your head' as the gunman, who was shoeless and in shorts, began running through the pool area shooting people at random. Some hotel staff managed to rush guests to safety as he approached them.

A hotel worker named Houssem told the Mail: ‘He was laughing as he was shooting. When he had finished and he had killed everyone, he did not care, he did not try to run. He was smiling, he was happy.’

By around 12.30pm local time he headed for back streets behind the hotel but armed police were now chasing him. A local builder threw a pile of tiles down on his head and ducked for cover. Rezgui started firing wildly as police shot back.

Tourists sheltered in shops as the gun fight continued before the terrorist was finally shot dead.

Rezgui, 23, murdered 38 people but he could have killed many more, police discovered after searching his body. An unexploded bomb was found on his person - with the detonator just inches away from his body.

He was described as polite and courteous by family and friends but although he didn't have a criminal record, Rezgui was known to authorities for 'low level radicalism'.

Speaking from the family home in Gaafour, a poor area of Tunisia's Saliana province, his uncle Ali Bin Muhammad Rezgui said: 'He was a sweet boy when he was younger. He was a breakdancer and loved football. He should have had a long, happy life.' 

Rezgui disguised his fanaticism by indulging in drinking and sex with girls at university, according to fellow students. His most recent exam results in engineering revealed he was one of the top performing students with a ‘very good’ attendance record.

But he was also plotting mass murder, apparently as part of a five-man sleeper cell completely unknown to police. Other members of the group allegedly went to wage holy war in Syria.

Detectives admitted they had no idea that ‘well-mannered’ Rezgui had been a jihadist for at least four years awaiting the call to arms.

In total, 30 Brits died in the attack, alongside three Irish citizens, two Germans and one national from each of Belgium, Russia and Portugal. 

Pictures of the aftermath were seen around the world: the bodies lying in the sand, the blood-stained swimming pool and Yacoubi, walking along the shore away from the carnage, his AK-47 slung casually at his side. 

The reverberations were felt across the country. The Foreign Office advises against all but essential travel to the country, and the number of British tourists visiting fell by 94 per cent in the first four months of 2016.

It is no surprise then that a third of Tunisia's hotels have shut down since last June, leaving thousands without work.

It is nothing short of disastrous for a country which relies on tourism for almost eight per cent of its GDP - and one which has sent more disillusioned, impoverished youth to join ISIS than any other nation on earth.

Tarek Aouadi, director of the Tunisian National Tourist Office in the UK, fought back tears as he begged for the travel advice to be reconsidered, and for people to return.

'Tunisia shouldn't be penalised because very hurtful, criminal people wanted to damage its economy,' he said.

'They are succeeding because if tourism is banned from the UK market that gives them a very good signal that they are on the winning side.'

For others, the tears flow for different reasons. 

Mehrez Saiidi was the general director of the Imperial, cannot forget the events of that Friday morning a year ago.

'We lost many of our friends on June 26,' he told MailOnline. 'We have good relationships with our clients: they were more like the relationship between friends, not like a hotel and their clients.

'So just imagine when you see one of your friends with blood, unable to move.

'I can still remember it vividly.'

The Imperial did try to reopen last autumn, determined not to be cowed by the hateful actions of one man and his warped ideology.

Barely any people came: in early October, there were just 30 guests. There should have as many as 800. 

It didn't matter though. Saiidi told SpeigelOnline: 'We are a symbol. The terrorists have not won as long as we are here.'

But now the doors are closed once more. It seemed like it might open again this month, but so far there is no sign of life in the marble-lined hallways.

It doesn't make Saiidi any less determined though. He, like Aouadi, believes European governments need to do more to encourage people make to Tunisia, which has employed more than 1,000 extra police officers to patrol its beaches and tourist destinations.

'The security situation in Tunisia is improving,' Saiidi said. 'But some European governments still consider it a black area and dangerous to their nationals.

'And it is more important now than ever that European countries help Tunisia.'

For now though, there is little he can do than wait, and remember the people who died.

'The anniversary on Sunday will be a celebration of life and a rejection of terrorism, to remember the lives of innocent people lost,' he said.

'Everything happened but we survived and we will continue we will not scare from the terrorism because this is what they looking for.

'We pray for the victims with every move we make in the Imperial Hotel.

'It's hard to remember but we survive and we will continue to survive.'
