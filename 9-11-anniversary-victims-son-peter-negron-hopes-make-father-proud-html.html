9/11 anniversary: Victim's son Peter Negron 'hopes he can make his father proud'

 Posted on June 9, 2024  |  5 minutes  |  905 words  |  Aldo Pusey

'I hope that I can make my father proud of the young men my brother and I have become': Boy who touched the nation's hearts honours dad who died in North Tower

As a 13-year-old boy he captured the nation's hearts with a rendition of the poem 'Stars', two years to the day after his father lost his life at the World Trade Center.

Today, Peter Negron stood up a 21-year-old man to emotionally honour his dad, Pete, and tell America how he has tried to teach his younger brother, Austin, who was just two at the time of the 9/11 attacks, lessons he had learned from their father.

Peter also said that losing his 'awesome' father, who worked on the 88th floor of the North Tower, spurred him to become a forensic scientist.

Powerfully emotive: Peter Negron, 21, addresses the 9/11 Memorial to honour his father, Pete, and tell the nation how the attacks made him want to become a forensic scientist

'Awesome father': Pete Negron, who died in the North Tower on 9/11 was today honoured by his son, Peter

'I have stopped crying, but I have not stopped missing my dad,' he said today, fighting back tears.

'My brother Austin had just turned two when he passed. I've tried to teach him all the things my father taught me: How to catch a baseball, how to ride, to always work hard in school; my dad always said how important it was.

'Since 9/11, my mother, brother and I moved to Florida. I got a job and enrolled in college. I wish my dad had been there to teach me how to drive, ask a girl out on a date, and see me graduate from high school. And a hundred other things I can't even begin to name.

'My father worked in the environmental department and cared about the Earth and our future. He wanted to make a difference. I admire him for that and I would have liked to talk to him about such things.

'I've decided to become a forensic scientist,' Peter said. 'I hope that I can make my father proud of the young men my brother and I have become.

'I miss you so much, Dad.'

Nine years on: A 13-year-old Peter Negron read the poem 'Stars' during the World Trade Center Memorial Service on September 11, 2003

Eight years ago, Peter stood on a stage at the World Trade Center on the two-year anniversary of the attacks, to read out a powerful tribute that touched the nation.

'I wanted to read you this because it says what I was feeling,' he said then.  'Stars. I like the way they look down from the sky and didn't seem to mind the way I cried.

'They didn't say wipe away those tears, or tell us, tell us what's the matter here.

'But shining through the dark, they calmly stayed and gently held me in their quiet way. I felt them watching over me, each one. It let me cry and cry until I was done.'

Pete Negron, from Bergenfield, New Jersey, was 34 when he died in the Twin Towers terrorist attack.

He had begun working in the World Trade Center in 1994, first as a draftsman with an architectural firm and then, on the 88th floor, as a Port Authority project manager for environmental issues.

Pete was so enamoured of the towers that he often took his son there to show off the building that touched the sky.

Keeping his memory alive: In 2002 Peter received an award for his father's bravery. He holds his brother Austin, then two years old, whom he has been telling stories of their father to ever since

Kiss: Peter's mother Leila Negron kisses Austin at the 2002 awards ceremony, as Peter, then 12, looks at his father's posthumous award

Then, on September 11, 2001, 10 days after Austin turned two, Leila Negron showed up at Roy W. Brown Middle School riddled with anxiety.

News of the plane crash was everywhere and Pete was not answering his phone.

'I kept hoping he'd come home that night,' Peter remembered in 2002. He and his mom tried to 'think positive', hopeful rescuers would find Pete somehow alive.

But one day months later, seven somber men and one woman approached their home and broke the horrible news.

'They gave me a cup of water to drink,' Peter said in 2002. 'I went to my room and closed the door and just stood there, for hours.'

The once delightfully mischievous boy, who had loved to clown around with friends, disappeared in a cocoon of solitude.

'For months after 9/11, Peter never smiled,' said Peter's guidance counsellor, Selma Alaimo in 2002. 'This sweet kid looked like he'd lost his very best friend.'

Peter today showed America that while he has not forgotten his best friend, he has come out of his cocoon and wants to dedicate his life to solving crime and helping to raise his 12-year-old brother.
