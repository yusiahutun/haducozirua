Cop killer loses appeal on life sentence after arguing it was cruel and unusual because of his age

 Posted on September 22, 2024  |  3 minutes  |  586 words  |  Larita Shotwell

The Missouri Court of Appeals has rejected a legal challenge by a convicted cop killer who argued that his life sentence was cruel and unusual punishment because he was just 18 years old when he committed the crime.

Trenton Forster, now aged 22, was high on drugs, angry and suicidal when he shot dead St Louis County police officer Blake Snyder as he approached the then 18-year-old's vehicle during a disturbance call in October 2016.

Forster was convicted in February 2019 of first-degree murder and was sentenced in April to life in prison without parole.

Forster's defense lawyers appealed the verdict, citing a state law that prohibits life sentences without parole for offenders younger than 18, reported St Louis Post-Dispatch.

On Tuesday, the state appeals court rebuffed Forster's argument. The decision was based on a recent state Supreme Court ruling that refused to raise the age limit for life sentences without the possibility of parole in first-degree murder cases.

As part of his appeal, Forster's legal team also claimed that some prejudicial evidence was introduced during his trial, including testimony from Snyder's widow, Elizabeth, an image of the 33-year-old slain officer in uniform and the defendant's anti-police rant from one of his jail phone calls. 

A jury convicted Forster last year of first-degree murder and other counts in connection to the October 2016 shooting 

At around 5am on October 6, 2016, police were called to a home in south St. Louis County. The caller said Forster, then 18, was banging on the door of a teenage girl’s home. Forster had a prior relationship with the girl.

Prosecutors said that when Snyder approached Forster, who by then was in a parked car, the officer said, 'Hey bud, let me see your hands.' According to testimony during the trial, Snyder's partner, Officer John Becker, then saw Snyder fall and fired at Forster, hitting him five times.

Snyder, who was married and the father of a 2-year-old boy, was pronounced dead at a hospital a short time later.

At Forster's trial, assistant prosecutor Alan Key Key told jurors that the defendant said to Becker, 'Shoot me, I have a gun,' though Forster’s pistol had jammed. 

In the week before the shooting, Forster tried multiple times to buy firearms and ammunition at gun stores and pawn shops, but was turned away several times because he appeared to be high. 

Eventually, someone he smoked marijuana with put him in touch with a private seller, who sold him a pistol and a rifle. A few days later he bought 50 rounds of ammunition at Walmart, Key said.

As part of his appeal, Forster's legal team claimed that some prejudicial evidence was introduced during his trial, including testimony from Snyder's widow, Elizabeth (pictured)

Public defender Stephen Reynolds had sought a second-degree murder conviction, maintaining that Forster did not think before shooting Snyder.

Reynolds also cited 'diminished capacity' because of Forster’s history of trauma, mental illness and drug use. A second-degree murder conviction would have carried a 30-year sentence with a possibility of parole.

During his sentencing, Forster apologized but also expressed anger at how poorly he thought prosecutors treated him.
