How to treat broken capillaries on face

 Posted on February 9, 2024  |  6 minutes  |  1221 words  |  Larita Shotwell

Broken capillaries can creep up on the most well cared-for complexion, no matter how forensically honed your routine may be. A common skin complaint, these tiny capillaries – known as spider or thread veins – appear as snaking red lines on the surface of the skin, a condition technically known as telangiectasia. Frustratingly, they’re notoriously tricky to keep under wraps, often glowing through a layer of concealer.

Thread veins can occur on skin of any type, tone and age. They appear when the tiny blood vessels just beneath the skin surface become dilated or broken, leading to fine, visible lines that are red, purple or even blue in colour.

What causes facial thread veins?

There are plenty of factors that can lead to the development of thread veins on the face, including several underlying health conditions. If you’re unsure of the cause of yours, it’s always best to consult a dermatologist, who can set you off on the right path.

Your network of capillary vessels is vital yet vulnerable. The wall of each capillary is thin, which allows for the absorption of nutrients and oxygen and the passing of waste. It's also elastic, which allows it to expand and contract. However, when a capillary is constantly expanding and contracting, it can eventually lose the ability to shrink back to size – sometimes rupturing completely – creating a visible appearance on the surface of the skin.

Below, see some of the most common causes of broken capillaries on the face.

What causes broken capillaries on the face?

Rosacea

Thread veins are a common side-effect of the chronic skin condition rosacea. An inflammatory skin disorder, rosacea prompts the blood vessels in the face to dilate, which is what leads to those recognisable red flushes. When rosacea is mild, any flushing will come and go quickly, but as the condition progresses the blood vessels can become dilated or broken, which leads to permanent broken capillaries on the surface.

Pressure

We all know not to prod and poke at breakouts, but it’s often easier said than done. Squeezing and applying physical pressure to the skin can stress the vascular network, leading to broken capillaries.

Temperature extremes

Shifting from warm to cold climes, and vice versa, can also contribute to the development of thread veins on the face. Our capillaries expand and contract according to the temperature, so repeated exposure to either extreme can stress the individual veins, reducing their ability to contract.

For this reason, it's best to avoid facials, therapies and at-home treatments that involve extreme temperatures: think cryotherapy and prolonged sauna sessions.

How to get rid of broken capillaries on the face

Unfortunately, there are no topical hacks for getting rid of telangiectasia. However, the good news is that a course of in-clinic treatments can make those visible broken veins disappear in entirety.

IPL has long been touted as the leading treatment for thread veins, but today’s most advanced clinics now prefer to use AFT: an advanced laser treatment that delivers better results with less of the discomfort.

“AFT stands for Advanced Fluorescence Technology,” explains Gosia Sokolowska, aesthetician at London’s facial clinic Pfeffer Sal. “It is the next generation of IPL systems: both use polychromatic light beams.” However, while the concept is the same, each system works in a different way, with AFT largely being considered the superior choice.

“In early IPL machines, some of the light was lost in the process,” explains Sokolowska. “New model AFT lasers recapture the lost light and convert it back into usable light which makes it more powerful in comparison to IPL.”

AFT works by emitting pulses of high-energy light that is converted into heat within the skin. This heat is absorbed by the pigment – in the case of thread veins, the red haemoglobin of the blood – which is then broken down and removed by the body in the days that follow.

Most clinics will recommend an average of four sessions of AFT for best results, with four weeks in-between bookings – although Sokolowska mentions that some of her clients are happy with their results after just a couple of sittings. Indeed the difference after just one is incredibly satisfying. And yes, there’s pain involved – a sensation akin to a hot elastic-band flick – but rest assured it is transient, and the laser’s in-built cooling tip quickly brings down any lingering heat.

Not everyone will be suitable for an AFT or IPL treatment: it’s limited to lighter skin between Fitzpatrick skin tones one-three, due to the way the laser works (it needs to be able to ‘see’ the contrasting pigment).

These laser treatments also cannot be performed on anyone with a suntan – real or otherwise – as the laser will pick up on the pigment, whether it’s actual melanin or a bottled bronze.

There’s no downtime involved with an AFT treatment, although you will need to treat your skin with care both before and after your appointment, meaning retinols and acids are off the cards for at-least a week either side. Sokolowska recommends being mindful of exposure to heat, avoiding intensive workouts and sun exposure, as well as keeping your skincare routine simple.

How to prevent broken capillaries

Be gentle

Physical pressure is a key cause of thread veins, so resist the temptation to pick, poke or scratch at your skin.

Choose strengthening skincare

Studies have found certain ingredients to be able to strengthen the capillary walls. Sokolowska recommends her clients incorporate a vitamin C or niacinamide product into their routines.

Limit your histamine

In some cases, facial redness is linked to a high level of histamine in the body. While this is of course tricky to control, you can try limiting your consumption of histamine-heavy foods and drinks: think long-matured cheeses, tomatoes, wine and champagne. Sokolowska adds that some “probiotics containing lactobacillus strains can also trigger histamine and rosacea flare-ups”.

How to conceal broken capillaries

There might not be a way to topically remove broken capillaries on the face, but you can mask their appearance with the right cosmetic products.

A green-tinged cream or colour-corrector is a brilliant way to mask more widespread redness. These products can be much more effective than a foundation or concealer, as they work to counterbalance the redness in your skin, rather than simply masking it. Clinique’s rosacea-targeting cream is brilliant for intermittent flushes, while Dr. Jart’s cult Cicapair cream contains calming centella asiatica alongside balancing green pigment.
