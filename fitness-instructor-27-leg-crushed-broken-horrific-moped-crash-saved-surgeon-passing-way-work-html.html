Fitness instructor whose leg was crushed is saved by passing surgeon

 Posted on June 2, 2024  |  6 minutes  |  1267 words  |  Reinaldo Massengill

A fitness instructor who almost lost her leg in a horrific moped crash was saved at the roadside by a passing surgeon on her way to work.

Colleen Wisdom, 27, had stopped at some traffic lights in Oxford city centre when a car drove into the back of her moped.

She was thrown into the air and ran over after the driver accidentally hit the clutch rather than the brake.

By chance, consultant plastic surgeon Sarah Tucker was at the scene - having taken a different route to her job at the city's John Radcliffe Hospital.

She started administering first aid immediately, saving Ms Wisdom's leg from amputation.

After being rushed to hospital and having multiple surgeries on her leg and back, Ms Wisdom recovered and is now thrilled to be walking again following two years of physiotherapy. 

Colleen Wisdom, 27, almost lost her leg in a horror road crash  (left) but was saved from amputation by a passing surgeon. After having surgery on her leg and back and physiotherapy she is now active again (right)

After being rushed to hospital following the crash, doctors worked for 14 hours to repair Ms Wisdom's leg. They sliced down the side of her limb, which is now a patchwork of skin grafts

Recalling the terrifying crash, Ms Wisdom said: 'The car in front was slowing down, and I had just come to a stop when I felt this huge bump. Then I was on the ground, staring up at the sky.

'I don't remember much, just horrendous pain. I was drifting in and out of consciousness. I remember thinking it must be a dream, but not understanding why the pain felt so real.'

The impact had severely fractured her left leg and thigh, crushed her left arm and caused the artery in the back of her knee to burst.

It left her with very little blood supply and caused the muscle to swell dangerously.

Whilst waiting for an ambulance, Mrs Tucker applied a tourniquet to Ms Wisdom's leg in two places – an action which is likely to have saved it from amputation.

'It must have been pretty grim. My bone had come clean out of my thigh. But Sarah was incredible,' said Ms Wisdom.

'She even calmed down passers-by, traumatised by seeing my injuries, as she worked.

'The ambulance men wanted her to travel with me, but she insisted on getting back on her bike, saying she'd get there quicker and wanted to clear theatre for me.'

Ms Wisdom was rushed to hospital, where a team of doctors – led by Mrs Tucker – worked for 14 hours to repair her leg.

Surgeons sliced down the side of her leg, which is now a patchwork of skin grafts, and also found that she had broken her back in three places.

The next day, she underwent a further six hours of surgery, this time one her back.

She was then sent to the hospital's intensive care ward, where she stayed for a week while recovering from surgery, and given ketamine to ease the pain

'I was completely out of it, to be honest,' she said. 'I remember absolutely nothing about that week. It was probably for the best.'

Ten days after the crash, Ms Wisdom was transferred to the hospital's trauma ward.

There, her partner Lisa Veal, 33, mother Kath Lidbury, 61, and step-dad Chris, 67, revealed to her the full extent of her shocking injuries. 

After the operation (right), doctors were not sure if Ms Wisdom would ever be able to walk again unaided. Formerly a fitness instructor (left), she found being in a wheelchair extremely difficult

While Ms Widsom has re-learned to walk, psychologically she still hates the sight of her legs, which she says are 'hugely scarred'

'A fortnight after the accident, nurses took off my bandages and I saw my leg for the first time. I burst into tears - I hadn't expected it to be as bad as it was,' said Ms Wisdom.

'My leg was a mess of skin grafts, a dark purple patchwork of criss-crossed skin, like Frankenstein.

'The skin all swollen and covered in pus. It was hideous. The dressings change took an hour and a half and I think I cried non-stop the whole time.'

In all, Ms Wisdom was in hospital for four weeks and two days.

I had a lucky escape. If it hadn't have been for Sarah passing when she did, I could have lost my leg and life would be very different Colleen Wisdom, 27 

Initially, doctors were unsure if she'd ever be able to walk without an aid, something she found extremely difficult as she came from a fitness background.

She said: 'My time in a wheelchair, not sure if I'd properly walk again, was a very dark time for me.

'But I went from weight-bearing, to hopping, to doing little jumps and, bit by bit, got my strength back.'

The two years of gruelling physiotherapy paid off, as Ms Wisdom was eventually able to walk unaided - and even do low-level aerobics classes.

She cannot play football or practice yoga any more due to two missing ligaments in her knee.

But while physically she is recovering well but still hates the sight of her leg.

She says her leg is 'hugely scarred' and so she doesn't ever show it, feeling nervous if she goes on holiday to places where people wear bikinis.  

She said: 'You'd think people would be more sensitive, but they stare, a lot. I'd prefer someone to come straight up and say, "What happened to your leg?" but the staring makes me very uncomfortable.'

Ms Wisdom, pictured with partner Lisa Veal, 33, says she now feels embarrassed as people star at her leg

Ms Wisdom, pictured at a wedding before her accident, said: 'I had a lucky escape. If it hadn't have been for Sarah passing when she did, I could have lost my leg and life would be very different'

She added that children have been scared by her scars, so skirts and shorts - which she used to wear all the time – are now resigned to the back of the wardrobe. 

Now, Ms Wisdom walks with just a slight limp, and has been so inspired by the people that helped her back on her feet that she is now considering a course in physiotherapy.

'Knock it all you like, but I have so much gratitude towards the NHS, from the amazing Dr Tucker to all the physiotherapists who helped me walk,' she said.

'I had a lucky escape. If it hadn't have been for Sarah passing when she did, I could have lost my leg and life would be very different.'

Dr Tucker, a member of British Association of Plastic Reconstructive and Aesthetic Surgeons, said: 'Coleen's experience really highlights the need for plastic surgeons at the sharp end of major trauma. We're just all so pleased to see the recovery she's made.

'Coleen and I did have a special bond by the time she walked out of hospital. I was there at the scene of the accident when everything seemed so helpless, and I was there to see her walk out of hospital unaided.

'That was a very moving moment, for both of us.'
